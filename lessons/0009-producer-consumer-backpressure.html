Lesson 9: Producer/Consumer and Backpressure

Objective: understand why real-time pipelines drop work instead of queueing it.

Key ideas

  • A camera produces ~30 frames/s forever; recognition might sustain 10/s. An unbounded queue therefore grows forever — latency climbs until the app shows results from seconds ago. This is the backpressure problem.
  • For live video the correct policy is "latest frame wins": old frames have no value once a newer one exists. A single-slot mailbox makes backlog structurally impossible rather than merely unlikely.
  • Count what you drop. A drop counter turns "it feels slow" into a measurable statement ("recognition keeps up with 40% of frames").
  • Worker error policy mirrors real operations: survive one bad frame (transient), stop loudly on persistent failure (structural), never loop silently on errors.
  • Shutdown is part of the design: a stop event plus join-with-timeout, and a loud failure if the thread will not die — leaked threads hold the camera hostage.

Retrieval question

Why is a size-1 slot better than a bounded queue of size 5 here? (Answer: any queued frame is already stale for display purposes; five queued frames just means acting on data five frames old. Only the newest frame matters.)
